### C41: Curvature-Dependent Cross-Section

**Physical question:** Does the cross-section for quantum-matter collision depend on local spacetime curvature?

**Physical reasoning:**
A spacetime quantum occupies a region of spacetime, and its effective size is set by the local geometry. In regions of high curvature (high matter density), the quantum is geometrically "compressed" — its spatial extent shrinks as the metric is distorted by nearby mass. The cross-section for collision therefore decreases in dense regions as sigma_eff = sigma / (1 + R/R_P) where R ~ 8*pi*G*rho_m is the local Ricci scalar from matter and R_P = c^4/(G*hbar) is the Planck curvature. This is the leading-order correction: in flat spacetime (low rho_m), sigma_eff -> sigma; in Planck-curved spacetime, sigma_eff -> 0.

**Equation:**

    dn_bar/dt + 3H*n_bar = Gamma_0 - sigma*n_bar*rho_m / (1 + 8*pi*G*rho_m/R_P)

where R_P = c^4/(G*hbar) ~ 1.6e70 m^-2 (Planck curvature).

**Key structural difference vs B1:** The annihilation term saturates at high rho_m: sigma_eff -> 0 as rho_m -> infinity. At cosmological densities (rho_m << R_P/(8*pi*G) = rho_Planck), the denominator is 1 and C41 is indistinguishable from B1. At high z this correction is negligible. This equation is IDENTICAL to B1 at all accessible redshifts.

**Dark energy prediction:** w0 ~ -0.9, wa ~ -0.1 (identical to B1). Curvature corrections appear only at rho_m ~ rho_Planck, inaccessible in cosmological history.

**DESI potential:** LOW (correction negligible at all observationally accessible densities)

**Extra parameters:** 0 (R_P is fixed by G and hbar)

### C42: Velocity-Dependent Cross-Section (Quantum Lorentz Factor)

**Physical question:** If spacetime quanta have a momentum distribution, does their relative velocity with matter affect the annihilation rate?

**Physical reasoning:**
A "collision" requires that the quantum and matter particle occupy the same location. The encounter rate between two species is proportional to their relative velocity: rate ~ n_bar * rho_m * |v_rel|. In the standard B1 derivation, v_rel is absorbed into sigma as a constant. But if spacetime quanta are generated at the Planck scale, their thermal velocity is set by the quantum foam temperature T_foam. As the universe expands, if quanta cool with the expansion (like photons: T_foam ~ 1/a), then v_rel ~ T_foam^{1/2} ~ a^{-1/2} ~ (1+z)^{1/2}. The effective cross-section then acquires a factor sigma_eff = sigma * (1+z)^{1/2}.

**Equation:**

    dn_bar/dt + 3H*n_bar = Gamma_0 - sigma*(1+z)^{1/2}*n_bar*rho_m

**Key structural difference vs B1:** The annihilation cross-section grows as (1+z)^{1/2}: annihilation is MORE efficient in the early universe due to higher quantum velocities. This suppresses n_bar at high z relative to B1, producing a steeper rho_DE(z) curve.

### C45: Probabilistic Annihilation (Survival Probability)

**Physical question:** What if not every collision leads to annihilation -- annihilation is probabilistic with probability p per collision?

**Physical reasoning:**
In the premise, "annihilation upon collision" need not be certain (probability 1). If the quantum must "resonate" with the matter particle's quantum state for absorption, the annihilation probability per encounter is p < 1. For p = const: this just rescales sigma -> p*sigma, identical to B1. The physical interest arises when p depends on local conditions. The most natural dependence: p is higher when the quantum energy matches the matter thermal energy (resonance condition). At high z, matter is hotter (T_m ~ (1+z)), quantum foam may be cooler or hotter, and the resonance probability p(z) varies. If the quantum foam cools with expansion as T_q ~ (1+z)^q and matter temperature T_m ~ (1+z), resonance probability is:

    p(z) = 1 - exp(-|T_q - T_m|^2 / T_ref^2)  [off-resonance is HIGH p when temperatures differ]

or the inverse (resonance = match):

    p(z) = exp(-(T_q(z) - T_m(z))^2 / T_ref^2)

The simplest tractable case: p depends on the ratio T_q/T_m. If quanta and matter both cool as (1+z), p = const = B1. If quanta cool differently (e.g., T_q ~ (1+z)^2), then p ~ exp(-(1+z)^2 * const). For the annihilation probability to INCREASE at high z (producing more suppression = more negative wa), we need p(z) increasing with z.

The minimal case with correct DESI direction: p(z) = 1 - exp(-tau_0*(1+z)^beta), where tau_0 is a dimensionless coupling. For tau_0 << 1: p ~ tau_0*(1+z)^beta, giving sigma_eff = sigma*tau_0*(1+z)^beta. This reduces to C42 for beta=1/2.

For beta = 1 (simplest integer):

    dn_bar/dt + 3H*n_bar = Gamma_0 - sigma*(1+z)*n_bar*rho_m

**Equation:**

    dn_bar/dt + 3H*n_bar = Gamma_0 - sigma*(1+z)*n_bar*rho_m

**Key structural difference vs B1:** Annihilation cross-section grows linearly with (1+z). This is STRONGER than C42's (1+z)^{1/2}. The equilibrium n_bar_eq ~ 1/(sigma*(1+z)*rho_m(z)+3H) drops faster at high z.

**Dark energy prediction:** wa more negative than C42, potentially closer to -0.83. w0 similar to B1 after Gamma_0 renormalization.

**DESI potential:** HIGH (direction correct, steeper than C42, no extra parameters if beta=1 is fixed by physical argument)

**Extra parameters:** 0 (if beta=1 taken as the natural integer; 1 if beta is free)

### C46: Minimum Exposure Time (Threshold Annihilation)

**Physical question:** What if a quantum must be "exposed" to matter for a minimum time tau_exp before annihilation can occur?

**Physical reasoning:**
Annihilation is not instantaneous. A quantum passing through a matter distribution must remain in the same spatial region as matter for a minimum time tau_exp ~ t_P (Planck time) before it is absorbed. In a smoothly expanding universe, the local matter density rho_m changes on timescale H^{-1}. The quantum sees a time-averaged density <rho_m> over its exposure window:

    <rho_m>_tau = (1/tau_exp) * integral_{t-tau_exp}^{t} rho_m(t') dt'

For tau_exp << H^{-1}: <rho_m>_tau ~ rho_m(t) -> B1 recovered.
For tau_exp ~ H^{-1}: the quantum sees a past-averaged density. Since rho_m grows going backwards in time (rho_m ~ a^{-3}), the effective rho_m is LARGER than the instantaneous value at time t. The quantum annihilates more readily because it was exposed to denser matter in the recent past.

For exponential memory with timescale tau_exp, the effective density is:
  rho_m_eff(t) = (1/tau_exp) * integral_0^inf rho_m(t-s) * exp(-s/tau_exp) ds

This is the auxiliary variable formulation:
  d(rho_m_eff)/dt = (rho_m - rho_m_eff)/tau_exp

The system:

    dn_bar/dt + 3H*n_bar = Gamma_0 - sigma*n_bar*rho_m_eff
    d(rho_m_eff)/dt = (rho_m - rho_m_eff) / tau_exp

For tau_exp -> 0: rho_m_eff -> rho_m -> B1.
For tau_exp ~ H0^{-1}: rho_m_eff(z=0) reflects weighted past. Since rho_m increased in the past, rho_m_eff > rho_m(today). This INCREASES annihilation today, requiring a larger Gamma_0 to compensate, potentially changing the z-evolution.

**Equation (2-ODE system):**

    dn_bar/dt + 3H*n_bar = Gamma_0 - sigma*n_bar*rho_m_eff
    d(rho_m_eff)/dt = (rho_m(t) - rho_m_eff) / tau_exp

**Key structural difference vs B1:** Two coupled ODEs. The effective matter density used in annihilation lags the actual density. At high z (going backward), the lag means n_bar sees a smaller effective density than B1 (it lags behind). This gives LESS suppression at high z -> wa is less negative. Wrong direction.

**Dark energy prediction:** wa less negative than B1 (lag underestimates past density). Wrong direction for DESI.

**DESI potential:** LOW (wrong wa direction for tau_exp > 0; reverts to B1 for tau_exp -> 0)

**Extra parameters:** 1 (tau_exp, but physically forced to be tau_exp ~ t_P << H0^{-1}, making it indistinguishable from B1)

### C48: Quantum Spectrum (Two-Population Annihilation)

**Physical question:** What if spacetime quanta have a spectrum of sizes -- short-lived (small, near matter) and long-lived (large, free)?

**Physical reasoning:**
The premise says quanta are annihilated by matter but says nothing about them being identical. Suppose there are two populations: "light" quanta (n_L) with cross-section sigma_L = sigma (normal) and "heavy" quanta (n_H) with cross-section sigma_H >> sigma (larger, annihilate more easily). The light quanta constitute the cosmological dark energy; the heavy quanta are rapidly depleted near matter and do not contribute to the background. In the homogeneous limit, both populations contribute to rho_DE but with different dynamics.

The simplest two-population model where only one parameter is added: parametrize by a fraction f of quanta with an enhanced cross-section sigma_H = alpha*sigma (alpha > 1):

    d(n_L)/dt + 3H*n_L = (1-f)*Gamma_0 - sigma*n_L*rho_m
    d(n_H)/dt + 3H*n_H = f*Gamma_0 - alpha*sigma*n_H*rho_m

    n_bar = n_L + n_H   [total contributes to rho_DE]

For alpha >> 1: n_H equilibrates much faster: n_H_eq ~ f*Gamma_0/(alpha*sigma*rho_m + 3H). At high z, the heavy population is suppressed as (alpha*sigma*rho_m)^{-1} ~ (1+z)^{-3}/alpha. At low z, the light population dominates. Total rho_DE has a two-component structure.

**Equation (effective single ODE for the dominant contribution at low z):**

    dn_bar/dt + 3H*n_bar = Gamma_0 - sigma_eff(z)*n_bar*rho_m

where sigma_eff(z) = sigma * [1 + f*(alpha-1)*sigma*rho_m / (alpha*sigma*rho_m+3H)] / [1 + f*(alpha-1)*sigma*rho_m / (alpha*sigma*rho_m+3H) * n_H_eq/n_bar_eq]

This reduces to an effectively running sigma: sigma_eff ~ sigma at low z (light population dominates) and sigma_eff ~ sigma*(1+delta) at high z (heavy population contributes more). Direction: sigma_eff increases at high z -> more suppression -> wa more negative.

**Compact two-parameter equation:**

    dn_bar/dt + 3H*n_bar = Gamma_0 - sigma*(1 + delta*(1+z)^gamma)*n_bar*rho_m

where delta parametrizes the heavy-population excess and gamma the spectral slope.

**Equation (1-parameter, gamma=1 fixed):**

    dn_bar/dt + 3H*n_bar = Gamma_0 - sigma*(1 + delta*(1+z))*n_bar*rho_m

**Key structural difference vs B1:** The effective cross-section grows linearly in (1+z), derived from a two-population physical picture without ad hoc z-dependence.

**Dark energy prediction:** wa more negative, w0 slightly shifted. The (1+z) factor increases suppression at high z. For delta ~ 0.1-1: wa could approach -0.83.

**DESI potential:** HIGH (physically motivated, 1 extra parameter that emerges from quantum spectrum picture)

**Extra parameters:** 1 (delta, the fractional amplitude of the heavy population; gamma=1 from the spectrum argument)

### C49: Clustered Quanta (Self-Shielding)

**Physical question:** If spacetime quanta cluster in clumps, do outer quanta shield inner ones from matter collisions?

**Physical reasoning:**
If spacetime quanta can gravitationally cluster (they have energy-momentum, so they respond to gravity), then their spatial distribution is not uniform. In regions of high quantum density, outer quanta can be annihilated first, shielding inner quanta from further collisions. The effective annihilation rate for a self-shielded cluster is suppressed by an optical-depth factor: rate_eff = rate_bare * exp(-tau_cluster) where tau_cluster = sigma*n_bar*l_cluster and l_cluster is the cluster size. For Planck-scale quanta: l_cluster ~ l_P, giving tau_cluster ~ sigma*n_bar*l_P ~ 4*pi*G*t_P*n_bar*l_P ~ 4*pi*(l_P/t_P)*t_P^2*n_bar ~ 4*pi*c*t_P^2*n_bar. This is Planck-suppressed. The shielding effect is negligible.

However, if clusters have cosmological scale (correlated with matter overdensities): l_cluster ~ lambda_J (Jeans length of quantum foam). For a pressure-less quantum gas: lambda_J is undefined or infinite. For quantum foam with Planck pressure: lambda_J ~ c_s / sqrt(G*rho_q) ~ c / sqrt(G*n_bar*mu). This is a fixed physical scale, not cosmologically evolving.

A minimal model capturing self-shielding without solving the full clustering problem: the effective annihilation rate saturates when n_bar exceeds a threshold n_sat (shielding kicks in):

    dn_bar/dt + 3H*n_bar = Gamma_0 - sigma*n_bar*rho_m / (1 + n_bar/n_sat)

For n_bar << n_sat: C49 -> B1. For n_bar >> n_sat: annihilation rate -> sigma*n_sat*rho_m (constant, independent of n_bar). This creates a runaway: n_bar grows without bound if Gamma_0 > sigma*n_sat*rho_m.

**Equation:**

    dn_bar/dt + 3H*n_bar = Gamma_0 - sigma*n_bar*rho_m / (1 + n_bar/n_sat)

**Key structural difference vs B1:** Annihilation saturates at high n_bar. This creates a bifurcation: for small Gamma_0, a stable equilibrium exists; for large Gamma_0, n_bar grows without bound. The dark energy would diverge -- physically interesting as a mechanism for DE domination but likely produces w < -1 (phantom).

**Dark energy prediction:** Can produce w0 << -1 (phantom crossing) if n_bar > n_sat. For n_bar << n_sat: identical to B1.

**DESI potential:** LOW (phantom crossing from back side; also n_sat is unconstrained without a clustering calculation)

**Extra parameters:** 1 (n_sat, the shielding threshold -- physically poorly constrained)

### C52: Total Energy Conservation (Covariant Continuity)

**Physical question:** What is the correct covariant form of energy conservation when quanta are annihilated?

**Physical reasoning:**
The covariant continuity equation for spacetime quanta in FLRW is not simply dn_bar/dt + 3H*n_bar = source. The energy density rho_q = n_bar*mu satisfies its own continuity equation separately from the number density:

    d(rho_q)/dt + 3H*(1+w_q)*rho_q = -sigma*n_bar*rho_m*mu + Gamma_0*mu

The number density equation is:
    dn_bar/dt + 3H*n_bar = Gamma_0 - sigma*n_bar*rho_m

These are consistent only if the quantum equation of state w_q is constant AND creation/annihilation preserves the energy per quantum (mu = const). If instead mu evolves (e.g., the energy per quantum is set by the Hubble scale: mu = hbar*H), then rho_q = n_bar*hbar*H and we must write the energy equation, not the number equation, as the fundamental one:

    d(rho_q)/dt + 3H*(1 + w_q)*rho_q = -sigma*(rho_q/mu)*rho_m*mu + Gamma_0*mu

With mu = hbar*H:
    d(n_bar*hbar*H)/dt + 3H*(1 + w_q)*n_bar*hbar*H = (Gamma_0 - sigma*n_bar*rho_m)*hbar*H

The H factors largely cancel, but dH/dt ~ -H^2*(3/2)*(1+w_eff) adds a correction:
    n_bar*(dH/dt) + H*(dn_bar/dt) + 3H^2*(1+w_q)*n_bar = (Gamma_0 - sigma*n_bar*rho_m)*H

    H*(dn_bar/dt) + 3H^2*(1+w_q)*n_bar = (Gamma_0 - sigma*n_bar*rho_m)*H - n_bar*dH/dt

Using dH/dt = -(3/2)*H^2*(1+w_eff) in the matter-dominated era:

### C55: Lifetime Distribution (Exponential Survival)

**Physical question:** Spacetime quanta have a creation-to-annihilation lifetime tau. What is the lifetime distribution P(tau) and how does it modify n_bar(t)?

    S(t | t_c) = exp(-sigma * integral_{t_c}^{t} rho_m(t') dt')

The number density at time t is:
    n_bar(t) = integral_{0}^{t} Gamma_0 * S(t | t_c) dt_c

Taking d/dt:
    dn_bar/dt = Gamma_0 - sigma*rho_m(t)*n_bar(t) - 3H*n_bar(t)

This EXACTLY recovers B1. The integral form and B1 are equivalent for Poissonian annihilation. So B1 already contains the full lifetime distribution physics -- no modification is needed.

However, if the lifetime has a MINIMUM (quanta cannot be annihilated below time tau_min after creation -- they need a "settling" period):
    S(t | t_c) = 0 for t < t_c + tau_min
    S(t | t_c) = exp(-sigma * integral_{t_c+tau_min}^{t} rho_m dt') for t >= t_c + tau_min

Then:
    n_bar(t) = integral_{0}^{t-tau_min} Gamma_0 * S(t | t_c) dt_c

This is a delayed annihilation: quanta created in the last tau_min are "immune." Taking d/dt:
    dn_bar/dt = Gamma_0 - Gamma_0*S(t | t-tau_min) - sigma*rho_m*n_bar - 3H*n_bar

For tau_min << H^{-1}: S(t | t-tau_min) ~ exp(-sigma*rho_m*tau_min) ~ 1 - sigma*rho_m*tau_min. So:
    dn_bar/dt = sigma*rho_m*tau_min*Gamma_0 - sigma*rho_m*n_bar - 3H*n_bar

**Equation (leading correction for tau_min << H^{-1}):**

    dn_bar/dt + 3H*n_bar = Gamma_0*(1 - exp(-sigma*rho_m*tau_min)) / (sigma*rho_m*tau_min) * sigma*rho_m*tau_min - sigma*rho_m*n_bar

    dn_bar/dt + 3H*n_bar = Gamma_0*(sigma*rho_m*tau_min) - sigma*rho_m*n_bar

**Key structural difference vs B1:** The effective creation rate is now proportional to rho_m (not constant). This is a significant change: Gamma_eff = Gamma_0*sigma*rho_m*tau_min ~ rho_m. The equilibrium:

At high z (sigma*rho_m >> 3H): n_bar_eq -> Gamma_0*tau_min = const. rho_DE is nearly constant at high z.
At low z (3H >> sigma*rho_m): n_bar_eq -> Gamma_0*tau_min*sigma*rho_m/(3H) -> 0.

This gives wa > 0 (more DE at high z, less at low z). Wrong direction for DESI.

**Dark energy prediction:** wa > 0 (more DE in the past). Wrong direction for DESI.

**DESI potential:** LOW (wrong wa sign)

### C57: Poisson Fluctuation Correction to Annihilation Rate

    <sigma*n*rho_m> = sigma * <n> * <rho_m> + sigma * <delta_n * delta_rho_m>

The last term is the covariance. For Poisson statistics: <(delta_n)^2> = n_bar (variance equals mean), so the correction from fluctuations in n alone gives:

    <sigma * n * (n-1) * rho_m/n_bar> != sigma*n_bar*rho_m in the case of two-body annihilation

For a SINGLE-body annihilation (one quantum, one matter particle): <rate> = sigma * <n> * <rho_m> = sigma * n_bar * rho_m. The Poisson fluctuation in n does not affect the mean rate.

However, the VARIANCE in the annihilation rate is sigma^2 * Var(n) * rho_m^2 = sigma^2 * n_bar * rho_m^2 (for Poisson n). This variance drives fluctuations in n_bar, but does not affect the mean ODE.

The correction appears if we use the LOG density instead of the linear density. The geometric mean <exp(log n)> != exp(<log n>) for a Poisson distribution. For a log-normal n with variance sigma_lnN^2:

    <n> = n_bar * exp(sigma_lnN^2/2)
    <n^2> = n_bar^2 * exp(2*sigma_lnN^2)

If the effective annihilation involves a non-linear rate f(n)*rho_m where f(n) is a nonlinear function, then <f(n)> != f(<n>). For f(n) = n^{1+epsilon} (slightly super-linear):

    <n^{1+epsilon}> = n_bar^{1+epsilon} * exp(epsilon*(1+epsilon)/2 * sigma_lnN^2)

For cosmic quanta undergoing clustering with variance sigma_lnN^2 = b_q^2 (quantum clustering bias):

    <rate> = sigma * n_bar^{1+epsilon} * rho_m * exp(epsilon*(1+epsilon)/2 * b_q^2)

The correction factor exp(...) depends on z through b_q(z). This is an effective renormalization of the annihilation rate. But epsilon = 0 for the standard linear mass-action law. To get a z-dependent correction, we need a physically motivated epsilon != 0.

The simplest non-trivial case: if quanta are BOSONS, they cluster (Bose-Einstein statistics enhances occupation in regions where many quanta already exist). The enhancement factor for Bose statistics is (1 + n_bar): rate_bosonic = sigma * n_bar * (1 + n_bar) * rho_m. For n_bar >> 1: rate ~ n_bar^2. For n_bar << 1: rate ~ n_bar -> B1.

**Equation:**

where n_ref is the reference density at which Bose enhancement becomes significant.

**Key structural difference vs B1:** The annihilation term has an extra n_bar/n_ref factor. At high z (when n_bar was larger), Bose enhancement increases the annihilation rate, suppressing n_bar more than B1. At low z (when n_bar has been depleted), the enhancement is weaker. This gives rho_DE that drops more at high z than at low z -- wa more negative.

**Dark energy prediction:** wa more negative than B1 (correct DESI direction). The strength depends on n_ref and n_bar(z).
